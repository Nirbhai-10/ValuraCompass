# 2. Target User Types and Personas

Each persona below is written as an operational brief: what to build for, what to never show, and what success looks like. Names are illustrative.

> Convention per persona: **Profile · Objectives · Problems with current tools · Complexity tolerance · Trust triggers · Friction triggers · Basic Mode needs · Advanced Mode needs · First view · Never want to feel · Conversion trigger · Retention trigger · Success definition.**

---

## Persona 1 — Riya, RIA running structured advisory

- **Profile.** 34, SEBI-registered RIA, 80 clients, fee-only, operates out of Bengaluru. Mix of mass affluent and mid-HNI. Runs 3–5 planning meetings/week.
- **Objectives.** Deliver a structured, repeatable planning process; document suitability rationale; produce defensible recommendations; reduce hours spent on deck-building.
- **Problems with current tools.** Excel sprawl, one-off PPT decks, no audit trail, no client portal, clients don't read long PDFs.
- **Complexity tolerance.** High.
- **Trust triggers.** Visible assumptions, override capability, exportable audit logs, suitability rationale captured automatically.
- **Friction triggers.** Having to re-enter data between Basic and Advanced; advisor-only screens that aren't actually advisor-only; AI text she can't edit before sending.
- **Basic Mode needs.** A prospect-conversion screen she can share in a 20-minute first call that already produces credible insights.
- **Advanced Mode needs.** Live-interview discovery flow, scenario engine, tax-aware observations, compliance-ready export.
- **First view.** Advisor dashboard with today's meetings, action center by household, open risk flags.
- **Never want to feel.** Embarrassed in front of a client by a glitchy screen or a naive number.
- **Conversion trigger.** A single meeting where the Basic Mode output impresses a prospect.
- **Retention trigger.** Annual review workspace that cuts her review-prep time in half.
- **Success.** 2× clients managed without 2× hours.

## Persona 2 — Vikas, Wealth manager handling affluent families

- **Profile.** 42, wealth manager at a bank-owned WM unit, 40 HNI families, Mumbai. Under quarterly review pressure.
- **Objectives.** Retain families, demonstrate planning depth beyond portfolio returns, structure conversations around goals and risk, not markets.
- **Problems with current tools.** Portfolio tools don't do planning; planning tools don't respect his clients' complexity (multiple entities, trusts, real estate, offshore).
- **Complexity tolerance.** High, but allergic to clutter in client meetings.
- **Trust triggers.** HNI-appropriate asset categories, estate/succession modules, proper NRI handling, premium aesthetic.
- **Friction triggers.** Client-facing screens that expose internal scores; anything that looks like a calculator.
- **Basic Mode needs.** A "health check" that surfaces gaps he can sell depth against.
- **Advanced Mode needs.** Full household map, scenario stress tests, tax-aware observations, succession readiness, estate documents tracking.
- **First view.** Family view with Household Intelligence Map and concentration risk callouts.
- **Never want to feel.** That the tool treats his clients like retail.
- **Conversion trigger.** A family meeting where the Household Intelligence Map lands.
- **Retention trigger.** Annual review deck branded for his firm.
- **Success.** Higher share of wallet; fewer families lost to multi-family offices.

## Persona 3 — Anita, Mutual fund distributor

- **Profile.** 38, AMFI-registered MFD, 600 folios across ~180 families, tier-2 city, hub-and-spoke practice.
- **Objectives.** Stop being perceived as a "fund seller"; reframe conversations around goals; increase SIP longevity and ticket sizes through planning context.
- **Problems with current tools.** Planning feels separate from her flow; long planning processes break her cadence.
- **Complexity tolerance.** Medium. Allergic to any workflow that adds >10 minutes per client.
- **Trust triggers.** Clean Basic Mode, India-specific instrument labels, respect for MFD realities (no pretense she's an RIA).
- **Friction triggers.** Being forced into compliance language she can't legally use.
- **Basic Mode needs.** A 10-minute household snapshot she can do on her phone in front of a client.
- **Advanced Mode needs.** Only unlocked on demand — mostly for her top 20 families.
- **First view.** Advisor dashboard with clients sorted by "action needed this month."
- **Never want to feel.** That the product pretends she's a licensed advisor.
- **Conversion trigger.** First time a Basic Mode output converts a transaction-only client into a planning conversation.
- **Retention trigger.** Monthly nudges that help her show up as proactive.
- **Success.** Longer SIP tenure, fewer lapses, higher cross-sell of protection.

## Persona 4 — Omar, Insurance advisor / protection specialist

- **Profile.** 45, insurance broker, half his book is endowment legacy, trying to move toward term-led conversations.
- **Objectives.** Show protection gaps with evidence, not pressure; surface adequacy numbers; shift the conversation from "this policy" to "this plan."
- **Problems with current tools.** Insurance illustrators are product-led; planning tools underweight insurance.
- **Complexity tolerance.** Medium.
- **Trust triggers.** Adequacy math that matches industry norms; endowment/ULIP honestly classified as savings-linked; nominee mapping taken seriously.
- **Friction triggers.** Forced product recommendations; sales language forced on him.
- **Basic Mode needs.** A protection gap number within 10 minutes, including family dependency context.
- **Advanced Mode needs.** Full adequacy engine, survivor income modeling, critical illness and disability coverage, policy-document OCR.
- **First view.** Protection adequacy dashboard with family dependency overlay.
- **Never want to feel.** Pushed into recommending specific products by the tool.
- **Conversion trigger.** A family meeting where the adequacy gap becomes emotionally obvious.
- **Retention trigger.** Renewal and life-event tracking that creates natural re-engagement.
- **Success.** Higher term:endowment mix in new business.

## Persona 5 — Sandeep, CA office combining tax + financial planning

- **Profile.** 50, partner in a 4-person CA firm, HNI and business-owner clients, Delhi.
- **Objectives.** Cross-sell planning to existing tax clients; produce an advisory deliverable beyond the ITR.
- **Problems with current tools.** Planning tools ignore tax nuance; tax tools ignore planning.
- **Complexity tolerance.** Very high.
- **Trust triggers.** Honest tax treatment, dual-regime awareness, no pretense of filing returns.
- **Friction triggers.** Generic tax tips; US-centric logic; anything that implies the tool files returns.
- **Basic Mode needs.** Quick snapshot at the end of an ITR consult.
- **Advanced Mode needs.** Full tax-aware observation engine, business-owner income modeling, promoter holdings, capital gains context, estate readiness.
- **First view.** Household view with tax observations and concentration risk.
- **Never want to feel.** That a software product is overreaching into tax opinion.
- **Conversion trigger.** Tax-aware observations that he genuinely agrees with.
- **Retention trigger.** Annual post-filing planning cycle that brings clients back.
- **Success.** A sustained advisory revenue line alongside tax.

## Persona 6 — Deepa, Research advisor

- **Profile.** 36, research advisor at a PMS firm, needs planning context to justify model portfolios for clients.
- **Objectives.** Ensure suitability of strategies given household context; document rationale.
- **Problems with current tools.** Suitability treated as a checkbox, not a living input.
- **Complexity tolerance.** High.
- **Trust triggers.** Suitability score derived from capacity + behavior + dependency, not just a risk quiz.
- **Friction triggers.** Suitability pretending to be a legal conclusion.
- **Basic Mode needs.** Fast suitability read plus risk profile input.
- **Advanced Mode needs.** Full suitability engine with audit-ready rationale.
- **First view.** Suitability + risk dashboard by household.
- **Never want to feel.** That suitability was generated without evidence.
- **Conversion trigger.** A suitability flag that catches a real mismatch.
- **Retention trigger.** Change logs that document evolution over time.
- **Success.** Fewer compliance exceptions.

## Persona 7 — Kabir, Self-serve young professional

- **Profile.** 28, tech job, INR 28 lakh package, 4 years into career, in a metro, no dependents yet, considering marriage.
- **Objectives.** Figure out if he's "on track"; avoid dumb mistakes; start investing meaningfully.
- **Problems with current tools.** Too many calculators, no unified view, no plain-English guidance.
- **Complexity tolerance.** Low initially, grows quickly with trust.
- **Trust triggers.** Clear UI, no hard-sell, plain English, honest uncertainty.
- **Friction triggers.** Long forms; scary numbers without context; product pitches.
- **Basic Mode needs.** A confidence-building snapshot in under 10 minutes.
- **Advanced Mode needs.** Optional depth when he's ready, with a nudge rather than a cliff.
- **First view.** Household snapshot with top 3 next actions.
- **Never want to feel.** Judged for low savings or high spending.
- **Conversion trigger.** First insight that tells him something he didn't know (e.g., emergency resilience is 0.6 months).
- **Retention trigger.** Quarterly check-ins that feel personal.
- **Success.** A few concrete actions completed in the first 90 days.

## Persona 8 — Maya & Arjun, Couple planning major goals

- **Profile.** Mid-30s, dual income, one child, considering a home purchase and children's education planning, Bengaluru.
- **Objectives.** Align on priorities; understand tradeoffs; reduce anxiety about affording it all.
- **Problems with current tools.** None support couple-mode decision-making.
- **Complexity tolerance.** Medium.
- **Trust triggers.** Couple view, joint/separate finances acknowledged, a shared decision surface.
- **Friction triggers.** A tool that only knows one user; surprises between two logins.
- **Basic Mode needs.** A couple-oriented Basic flow where both can answer from their own devices.
- **Advanced Mode needs.** Joint goals, scenario sliders, protection and education modeling.
- **First view.** Couple dashboard with joint goals, combined cash flow, shared action center.
- **Never want to feel.** Conflict amplified by the tool.
- **Conversion trigger.** First time a home-affordability scenario becomes clear.
- **Retention trigger.** Annual re-check that tracks progress.
- **Success.** A decision made together on record.

## Persona 9 — Priya, NRI / cross-border family

- **Profile.** 39, Senior manager in Dubai, Indian citizen, spouse and two children in Dubai, parents in Chennai, owns a flat in Bengaluru (rented), has NRE/NRO accounts, plans to return in 7–10 years.
- **Objectives.** Balance GCC savings with Indian obligations; plan parents' healthcare; prepare for eventual return.
- **Problems with current tools.** Tax-residency ignored, currency mixing broken, parents' support ignored, return planning absent.
- **Complexity tolerance.** High.
- **Trust triggers.** Residency vs tax residency modeled separately; currency and jurisdiction visible; NRE/NRO/FCNR recognized.
- **Friction triggers.** A tool that assumes she lives in India.
- **Basic Mode needs.** Fast cross-border snapshot with residency implications highlighted.
- **Advanced Mode needs.** Full cross-border modeling, repatriation planning, parents' care modeling, multi-currency goals.
- **First view.** Household view with residency, currency exposure, and obligations panel.
- **Never want to feel.** Misclassified.
- **Conversion trigger.** First cross-border insight that feels accurate.
- **Retention trigger.** Relocation planning before her return.
- **Success.** Successful return transition.

## Persona 10 — The Iyer family supporting elderly parents

- **Profile.** Primary earners Ramesh (44) and Latha (42), two kids, both parents of Ramesh live with them, mother has a chronic condition.
- **Objectives.** Make sure parents' healthcare is funded; balance kids' education; understand insurance adequacy.
- **Problems with current tools.** Eldercare is a blank spot.
- **Complexity tolerance.** Medium.
- **Trust triggers.** Eldercare planning as a first-class module; healthcare reserve modeled; senior citizen health cover recognized.
- **Friction triggers.** Tools that treat parents as "other expenses."
- **Basic Mode needs.** Parental obligation captured in 2 questions.
- **Advanced Mode needs.** Healthcare reserve, caregiver burden, critical illness modeling, senior cover tracking.
- **First view.** Family dependency map with eldercare highlighted.
- **Never want to feel.** Guilty; ambushed.
- **Conversion trigger.** First insight that explains healthcare reserve concretely.
- **Retention trigger.** Life-event tracker tied to parents' health.
- **Success.** A clear, funded plan for eldercare.

## Persona 11 — The Shah family with a special-needs dependent

- **Profile.** Parents 46 and 44, two children, elder child has a disability requiring lifelong support.
- **Objectives.** Plan for the child's lifelong care; ensure continuity if parents are unavailable; structure a trust/nominee plan; obtain appropriate insurance and benefits.
- **Problems with current tools.** Special-needs planning is absent.
- **Complexity tolerance.** High when the tool acknowledges the reality.
- **Trust triggers.** Dedicated special-needs planning blocks; continuity/guardianship modeling; avoidance of ableist language.
- **Friction triggers.** Being forced into "retirement first, everything else second."
- **Basic Mode needs.** Special-needs dependent captured as a first-class household member with no friction.
- **Advanced Mode needs.** Lifetime care cost modeling, trust/guardianship readiness, insurance adequacy including caregiver loss.
- **First view.** Household map with dependency flagged; dedicated module link.
- **Never want to feel.** Othered.
- **Conversion trigger.** Seeing a tool that sees their reality.
- **Retention trigger.** Continuity planning updates over time.
- **Success.** A defensible continuity plan on record.

## Persona 12 — Rohit, Business-owner / promoter household

- **Profile.** 49, owns a manufacturing company (promoter, ~70% stake), two children, wife co-director, significant illiquid net worth, multi-entity structure.
- **Objectives.** Separate household wealth from business dependency; plan succession; fund education; structure protection.
- **Problems with current tools.** Treat business as "other income"; ignore promoter dependency risk.
- **Complexity tolerance.** Very high.
- **Trust triggers.** Business income modeling, concentration risk explicit, succession readiness module.
- **Friction triggers.** Oversimplification.
- **Basic Mode needs.** Promoter dependency flagged in the first pass.
- **Advanced Mode needs.** Full business-household modeling, succession map, keyman and business protection observations.
- **First view.** Household view with concentration + succession flags front and center.
- **Never want to feel.** That the product is built for salaried employees only.
- **Conversion trigger.** First scenario showing household resilience if the business underperforms.
- **Retention trigger.** Annual succession and estate readiness review.
- **Success.** A separated household financial identity from the business.

## Persona 13 — HNI / UHNI household (family office candidate)

- **Profile.** Net worth INR 50+ crore, multiple earners, trusts, offshore entities, private investments, multiple properties, staff.
- **Objectives.** Consolidated household visibility, multi-generation planning, legacy structuring, risk governance.
- **Complexity tolerance.** Maximum.
- **Trust triggers.** Multi-entity ownership modeling, private investments first-class, legacy planning, offshore residency logic.
- **Friction triggers.** Retail framing; simplistic severity language.
- **Basic Mode needs.** An elegant executive snapshot for the principal; data entry delegated to the advisor.
- **Advanced Mode needs.** Full depth, entity ownership, trust flags, family office-grade aggregation, custom assumption sets.
- **First view.** Household executive view with legacy, risk governance, and liquidity panels.
- **Never want to feel.** That the product is retail software with a premium skin.
- **Conversion trigger.** The first executive one-pager that respects their scale.
- **Retention trigger.** Annual governance review + life-event handling.
- **Success.** Compass becomes the household's planning system of record.

## Persona 14 — Advisor in a quick prospect-conversion meeting

- **Profile.** Any of the advisor personas above, but under time pressure with a prospective client.
- **Objectives.** Earn the next meeting within 20 minutes; demonstrate value fast.
- **Trust triggers.** Basic Mode that is beautiful, honest, and produces one memorable insight.
- **Friction triggers.** Anything that takes longer than the time in the meeting.
- **Needs.** Basic Mode with a "shareable one-pager" at the end and a single call-to-action for a follow-up Advanced session.
- **Success.** Conversion rate from Basic Mode session to follow-up meeting.

## Persona 15 — Senior planner in a long live planning interview

- **Profile.** Senior advisor running a 90–150 minute live planning interview with a family.
- **Objectives.** Comprehensive discovery; surface non-obvious dependencies; leave the session with a drafted plan and a task list.
- **Trust triggers.** Advanced Mode that can be driven conversationally; ability to jump modules; Meeting Mode that hides clutter.
- **Friction triggers.** Getting boxed into a linear form.
- **Needs.** Jump-navigation across all Advanced modules; on-screen summarization panel; live task creation; advisor notes layer; export to compliance-ready file.
- **Success.** A defensible plan and action log produced in the session itself, not "later."
